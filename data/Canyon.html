<p>The known <b>history of the Grand Canyon area</b> stretches back 10,500 years, when the first evidence of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> presence in the area is found. <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> have inhabited the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a> and the area now covered by <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_National_Park" title="Grand Canyon National Park">Grand Canyon National Park</a> for at least the last 4,000 of those years. <a href="/wiki/Anasazi" title="Anasazi" class="mw-redirect">Anasazi</a>, first as the Basketmaker culture and later as the more familiar Puebloans, developed from the Desert Culture as they became less <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomadic</a> and more dependent on <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. A similar culture, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cohonina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cohonina (page does not exist)">Cohonina</a>, also lived in the canyon area. <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">Drought</a> in the late 13th century likely caused both groups to move on. Other peoples followed, including the <a href="/wiki/Paiute" title="Paiute" class="mw-redirect">Paiute</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerbat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cerbat (page does not exist)">Cerbat</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Nation" title="Navajo Nation">Navajo</a>, only to be later forced onto <a href="/wiki/Indian_reservation" title="Indian reservation">reservations</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Government" title="United States Government" class="mw-redirect">United States Government</a>.</p>
<p>In September 1540, under direction by <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a> <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Vasquez_de_Coronado" title="Francisco Vasquez de Coronado" class="mw-redirect">Francisco Vasquez de Coronado</a> to find the fabled <a href="/wiki/Seven_Cities_of_Cibola" title="Seven Cities of Cibola" class="mw-redirect">Seven Cities of Cibola</a>, Captain <a href="/wiki/Garcia_Lopez_de_Cardenas" title="Garcia Lopez de Cardenas" class="mw-redirect">Garcia Lopez de Cardenas</a> led a party of Spanish soldiers with <a href="/wiki/Hopi" title="Hopi">Hopi</a> guides to the Grand Canyon. Over 200 years passed before two Spanish priests became the second party of non-Native Americans to see the canyon. U.S. Army Major <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley_Powell" title="John Wesley Powell">John Wesley Powell</a> led the 1869 <a href="/wiki/Powell_Geographic_Expedition" title="Powell Geographic Expedition" class="mw-redirect">Powell Geographic Expedition</a> through the canyon on the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River_(US)" title="Colorado River (US)" class="mw-redirect">Colorado River</a>. This and later study by geologists uncovered the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area" title="Geology of the Grand Canyon area">geology of the Grand Canyon area</a> and helped to advance that science. In the late 19th century, the promise of mineral resources—mainly <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>—renewed interest in the region. The first pioneer settlements along the rim came in the 1880s.</p>
<p>Early residents soon realized that <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> was destined to be more profitable than <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, and by the turn of the century the Grand Canyon was a well-known tourist destination. Most visitors made the grueling trip from nearby towns to the South Rim by <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach" title="Stagecoach">stagecoach</a>. In 1901 the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_Railway" title="Grand Canyon Railway">Grand Canyon Railway</a> was opened from <a href="/wiki/Williams,_Arizona" title="Williams, Arizona">Williams, Arizona</a>, to the South Rim, and the development of formal tourist facilities, especially at <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_Village,_Arizona" title="Grand Canyon Village, Arizona">Grand Canyon Village</a>, increased dramatically. The <a href="/wiki/Fred_Harvey_Company" title="Fred Harvey Company">Fred Harvey Company</a> developed many facilities at the Grand Canyon, including the luxury <a href="/wiki/El_Tovar_Hotel" title="El Tovar Hotel">El Tovar Hotel</a> on the South Rim in 1905 and Phantom Ranch in the Inner Gorge in 1922. Although first afforded federal protection in 1893 as a forest reserve and later as a <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Monument" title="U.S. National Monument" class="mw-redirect">U.S. National Monument</a>, the Grand Canyon did not achieve <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Park" title="U.S. National Park" class="mw-redirect">U.S. National Park</a> status until 1919, three years after the creation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>. Today, Grand Canyon National Park receives about five million visitors each year, a far cry from the annual visitation of 44,173 in 1919.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Native American inhabitation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Native_American_inhabitation">Native American inhabitation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spilt-twig_figurine_from_the_Grand_Canyon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Spilt-twig_figurine_from_the_Grand_Canyon.jpg/220px-Spilt-twig_figurine_from_the_Grand_Canyon.jpg" width="220" height="307" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spilt-twig_figurine_from_the_Grand_Canyon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Split-twig figurine from the Grand Canyon</div>

<p>Current archaeological evidence suggests that humans inhabited the Grand Canyon area as far back as 4,000 years ago<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and at least were passers-through for 6,500 years before that.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> of artifacts found in <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> caves in the inner canyon indicate ages of 3,000 to 4,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In the 1930s split-twig animal figurines were found in the Redwall Limestone cliffs of the Inner Gorge that were dated in this range. These animal figurines are a few inches (7 to 8&#160;cm) in height and made primarily from twigs of <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Populus_sect._Aegiros" title="Populus sect. Aegiros">cottonwood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> This and other evidence suggests that these inner canyon dwellers were part of Desert Culture; a group of semi-nomadic hunter-gatherer Native Americans.</p>
<p>The Basketmaker Anasazi (also called the Histatsinom, meaning "people who lived long ago") evolved from the Desert Culture sometime around 500&#160;BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> This group inhabited the rim and inner canyon and survived by <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunting and gathering</a> along with some limited agriculture. Noted for their basketmaking skills (hence their name), they lived in small communal bands inside <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a> and circular mud structures called <a href="/wiki/Pithouse" title="Pithouse" class="mw-redirect">pithouses</a>. Further refinement of agriculture and technology led to a more sedentary and stable lifestyle for the Anasazi starting around 500&#160;CE.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Contemporary with the flourishing of Anasazi culture, another group, called the Cohonina lived west of the current site of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_Village" title="Grand Canyon Village" class="mw-redirect">Grand Canyon Village</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Anasazi in the Grand Canyon area started to use <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a> in addition to mud and poles to erect above-ground houses sometime around 800&#160;CE.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Thus the Pueblo period of Anasazi culture was initiated. In summer, the Puebloans migrated from the hot inner canyon to the cooler high plateaus and reversed the journey for winter.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Large graineries and multi-room <a href="/wiki/Pueblo" title="Pueblo">pueblos</a> survive from this period. There are around 2,000 known Anasazi archaeological sites in park boundaries. The most accessible site is <a href="/wiki/Tusayan_Ruins" title="Tusayan Ruins">Tusayan Pueblo</a>, which was constructed sometime around 1185 and housed 30 or so people.<sup id="cite_ref-APN_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APN-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tusayan_at_the_Grand_Canyon-storage_areas.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Anasazi food storage building ruins at Tusayan Pueblo</div>

<p>Large numbers of dated archaeological sites indicate that the Anasazi and the Cohonina flourished until about 1200&#160;CE.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Something happened a hundred years after that, however, that forced both of these cultures to move away. Several lines of evidence led to a theory that <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> caused a severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> in the region from 1276 to 1299, forcing these agriculture-dependent cultures to move on.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Many Anasazi relocated to the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Little_Colorado_River" title="Little Colorado River">Little Colorado River</a> drainages, where their descendants, the <a href="/wiki/Hopi" title="Hopi">Hopi</a> and the <a href="/wiki/19_Pueblos_of_New_Mexico" title="19 Pueblos of New Mexico" class="mw-redirect">19 Pueblos of New Mexico</a>, now live.<sup id="cite_ref-APN_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APN-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The Hopi people believe they emerged from the canyon and that their spirits rest here.</p>
<p>For approximately one hundred years the canyon area was uninhabited by humans.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paiute" title="Paiute" class="mw-redirect">Paiute</a> from the east and Cerbat from the west were the first humans to reestablish settlements in and around the Grand Canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The Pauite settled the <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateaus</a> north of the Colorado River and the Cerbat built their communities south of the river, on the <a href="/wiki/Coconino_Plateau" title="Coconino Plateau">Coconino Plateau</a>. Sometime in the 15th century the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Nation" title="Navajo Nation">Navajo</a>, or the Diné, arrived in the area.</p>
<p>All three cultures were stable until the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> moved them to <a href="/wiki/Indian_reservation" title="Indian reservation">Indian reservations</a> in 1882 as part of the removal efforts that ended the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Wars" title="Indian Wars" class="mw-redirect">Indian Wars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Havasupai" title="Havasupai">Havasupai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hualapai" title="Hualapai">Hualapai</a> are descended from the Cerbat and still live in the immediate area. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Havasu_Village&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Havasu Village (page does not exist)">Havasu Village</a>, in the western part of the current park, is likely one of the oldest continuously occupied settlements in the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Adjacent to the eastern part of the park is the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Nation" title="Navajo Nation">Navajo Nation</a>, the largest reservation in the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historic exploration">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_exploration">Historic exploration</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spanish">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">Spanish</span></h3>
<p>The first Europeans reached the Grand Canyon in September 1540.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It was a group of about 13 Spanish soldiers led by <a href="/wiki/Garc%C3%ADa_L%C3%B3pez_de_C%C3%A1rdenas" title="García López de Cárdenas">García López de Cárdenas</a>, dispatched from the army of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_V%C3%A1squez_de_Coronado" title="Francisco Vásquez de Coronado">Francisco Vásquez de Coronado</a> on its quest to find the fabulous <a href="/wiki/Seven_Cities_of_Cibola" title="Seven Cities of Cibola" class="mw-redirect">Seven Cities of Cibola</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Casta.C3.B1eda1596_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casta.C3.B1eda1596-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The group was led by <a href="/wiki/Hopi" title="Hopi">Hopi</a> guides and, assuming they took the most likely route, must have reached the canyon at the South Rim, probably between today's Desert View and Moran Point.</p>
<p>The report indicates that they greatly misjudged the proportions of the gorge. On the one hand, they estimated that the canyon was about three to four <a href="/wiki/League_(unit)" title="League (unit)">leagues</a> wide (13–16&#160;km, 8–10&#160;mi), which is quite accurate.<sup id="cite_ref-Casta.C3.B1eda1596_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casta.C3.B1eda1596-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> At the same time, however, they believed that the river, which they could see from above, was only 2&#160;m (6&#160;ft) wide (in reality it is about a hundred times wider).<sup id="cite_ref-Casta.C3.B1eda1596_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casta.C3.B1eda1596-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Being in dire need of water, and wanting to cross the giant obstacle, the soldiers started searching for a way down to the canyon floor that would be passable for them along with their horses. After three full days, they still hadn't been successful, and it is speculated that the Hopi, who probably knew a way down to the canyon floor, were reluctant to lead them there.<sup id="cite_ref-Casta.C3.B1eda1596_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casta.C3.B1eda1596-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As a last resort, Cárdenas finally commanded the three lightest and most agile men of his group to climb down by themselves (their names are given as Pablo de Melgosa, Juan Galeras, and an unknown, third soldier).<sup id="cite_ref-Casta.C3.B1eda1596_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casta.C3.B1eda1596-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> After several hours, the men returned, reporting that they had only made one third of the distance down to the river, and that "what seemed easy from above was not so".<sup id="cite_ref-Casta.C3.B1eda1596_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casta.C3.B1eda1596-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Furthermore, they claimed that some of the boulders which they had seen from the rim, and estimated to be about as tall as a man, were in fact bigger than the <a href="/wiki/Giralda" title="Giralda">Great Tower of Seville</a> (which then was the tallest building in the world, measuring 82 metres, or 270 feet).<sup id="cite_ref-greatabyss_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greatabyss-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Cárdenas finally had to give up and returned to the main army. His report of an impassable barrier forestalled further visitation to the area for two hundred years.</p>
<p>Only in 1776 did two Spanish Priests, Fathers <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Atanasio_Dom%C3%ADnguez" title="Francisco Atanasio Domínguez">Francisco Atanasio Domínguez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silvestre_V%C3%A9lez_de_Escalante" title="Silvestre Vélez de Escalante">Silvestre Vélez de Escalante</a> travel along the North Rim again, together with a group of Spanish soldiers, exploring southern Utah in search of a route from <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe, New Mexico</a> to <a href="/wiki/Monterey,_California" title="Monterey, California">Monterey, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p12_0-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p12-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in 1776, Fray Francisco Garces, a Franciscan missionary, spent a week near Havasupai, unsuccessfully attempting to convert a band of Native Americans. He described the canyon as "profound".<sup id="cite_ref-greatabyss_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greatabyss-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Americans">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Americans">Americans</span></h3>
<p>James Ohio Pattie and a group of American trappers and mountain men were probably the next Europeans to reach the canyon in 1826.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> There is little in terms of documentation to support this, however.</p>
<p>The signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Guadalupe_Hidalgo" title="Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo">Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo</a> in 1848 ceded the Grand Canyon region to the United States. <a href="/wiki/Jules_Marcou" title="Jules Marcou">Jules Marcou</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Railroad_Survey" title="Pacific Railroad Survey" class="mw-redirect">Pacific Railroad Survey</a> made the first geologic observations of the canyon and surrounding area in 1856.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Jacob_Hamblin" title="Jacob Hamblin">Jacob Hamblin</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Mormon" title="Mormon">Mormon</a> missionary) was sent by <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young" title="Brigham Young">Brigham Young</a> in the 1850s to locate easy river crossing sites in the canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Hamblin1971_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin1971-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Building good relations with local <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> and white settlers, he discovered <a href="/wiki/Lee%27s_Ferry" title="Lee's Ferry">Lee's Ferry</a> in 1858 and <a href="/wiki/Pierce_Ferry" title="Pierce Ferry" class="mw-redirect">Pierce Ferry</a> (later operated by, and named for, Harrison Pierce)—the only two sites suitable for ferry operation.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1857 <a href="/wiki/Edward_Fitzgerald_Beale" title="Edward Fitzgerald Beale">Edward Fitzgerald Beale</a> led an expedition to survey a wagon road from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Defiance,_Arizona" title="Fort Defiance, Arizona">Fort Defiance, Arizona</a> to the Colorado River.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> On September 19 near present day National Canyon they came upon what May Humphreys Stacey described in his journal as "...a wonderful canyon four thousand feet deep. Everyone (in the party) admitted that he never before saw anything to match or equal this astonishing natural curiosity."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/U.S._War_Department" title="U.S. War Department" class="mw-redirect">U.S. War Department</a> expedition led by Lt. Joseph Ives was launched in 1857 to investigate the area's potential for natural resources, to find railroad routes to the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">west coast</a>, and assess the feasibility of an up-river navigation route from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The group traveled in a stern wheeler <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamboat</a> named <i>Explorer</i>. After two months and 350 miles (560&#160;km) of difficult navigation, his party reached Black Canyon some two months after George Johnson.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> In the process, the <i>Explorer</i> struck a rock and was abandoned. The group later traveled eastwards along the South Rim of the Grand Canyon.</p>

<p>A man of his time, Ives discounted his own impressions on the beauty of the canyon and declared it and the surrounding area as "altogether valueless", remarking that his expedition would be "the last party of whites to visit this profitless locality".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Attached to Ives' expedition was geologist <a href="/wiki/John_Strong_Newberry" title="John Strong Newberry">John Strong Newberry</a> who had a very different impression of the canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> After returning, Newberry convinced fellow geologist <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley_Powell" title="John Wesley Powell">John Wesley Powell</a> that a boat run through the Grand Canyon to complete the survey would be worth the risk.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris1997_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris1997-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>note 1<span>]</span></a></sup> Powell was a major in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and was a veteran of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, a conflict that cost him his right forearm in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shiloh" title="Battle of Shiloh">Battle of Shiloh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>More than a decade after the Ives Expedition and with help from the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>, Powell led the first of the <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley_Powell#Expeditions" title="John Wesley Powell">Powell Expeditions</a> to explore the region and document its scientific offerings. On May 24, 1869, the group of nine men set out from Green River Station in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> down the Colorado River and through the Grand Canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p396_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p396-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> This first expedition was poorly funded and consequently no <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photographer</a> or graphic <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">artist</a> was included. While in the Canyon of Lodore one of the group's four boats capsized, spilling most of their food and much of their scientific equipment into the river. This shortened the expedition to one hundred days. Tired of being constantly cold, wet and hungry and not knowing they had already passed the worst rapids, three of Powell's men climbed out of the canyon in what is now called Separation Canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p397_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p397-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Once out of the canyon, all three were reportedly killed by Shivwits band <a href="/wiki/Paiute" title="Paiute" class="mw-redirect">Paiutes</a> who thought they were miners that recently molested and killed a female Shivwit.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p397_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p397-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> All those who stayed with Powell survived and that group successfully ran most of the canyon.</p>

<p>Two years later a much better-funded Powell-led party returned with redesigned boats and a chain of several supply stations along their route. This time, photographer E.O. Beaman and 17-year-old artist Frederick Dellenbaugh were included.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p397_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p397-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Beaman left the group in January 1872 over a dispute with Powell and his replacement, James Fennemore, quit August that same year due to poor health, leaving boatman <a href="/wiki/John_Karl_Hillers" title="John Karl Hillers">John K. Hillers</a> as the official photographer (nearly one ton of photographic equipment was needed on site to process each shot).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Famed painter <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Moran" title="Thomas Moran">Thomas Moran</a> joined the expedition in the summer of 1873, after the river voyage and thus only viewed the canyon from the rim. His 1873 painting "Chasm of the Colorado" was bought by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> in 1874 and hung in the lobby of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Powell expeditions systematically cataloged rock <a href="/wiki/Formation_(geology)" title="Formation (geology)" class="mw-redirect">formations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, animals, and archaeological sites. Photographs and illustrations from the Powell expeditions greatly popularized the canyonland region of the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_United_States" title="Southwest United States" class="mw-redirect">southwest United States</a>, especially the Grand Canyon (appreciating this, Powell added increasing resources to that aspect of his expeditions). Powell later used these photographs and illustrations in his lecture tours, making him a national figure. Rights to reproduce 650 of the expeditions' 1,400 <a href="/wiki/Stereograph" title="Stereograph" class="mw-redirect">stereographs</a> were sold to help fund future Powell projects.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1881 he became the second director of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Geological_Survey" title="U.S. Geological Survey" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Geological Survey</a>.</p>

<p>Geologist <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Dutton" title="Clarence Dutton">Clarence Dutton</a> followed up on Powell's work in 1880–1881 with the first in-depth geological survey of the newly formed U.S. Geological Survey.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Painters Thomas Moran and William Henry Holmes accompanied Dutton, who was busy drafting detailed descriptions of the area's geology. The report that resulted from the team's effort was titled <i>A Tertiary History of The Grand Canyon District, with Atlas</i> and was published in 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> This and later study by geologists uncovered the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area" title="Geology of the Grand Canyon area">geology of the Grand Canyon area</a> and helped to advance that science. Both the Powell and Dutton expeditions helped to increase interest in the canyon and surrounding region.</p>
<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown-Stanton_expedition&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brown-Stanton expedition (page does not exist)">Brown-Stanton expedition</a> was started in 1889 to survey the route for a "water-level" railroad line through the canyons of the Colorado River to the Gulf of California.<sup id="cite_ref-Lohman1981_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lohman1981-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The proposed Denver, Colorado Canyon, and Pacific Railway was to carry coal from mines in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. Expedition leader Frank M. Brown, his chief engineer Robert Brewster Stanton, and 14 other men set out in six boats from <a href="/wiki/Green_River,_Utah" title="Green River, Utah">Green River, Utah</a>, on May 25, 1889.<sup id="cite_ref-Lohman1981_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lohman1981-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Brown and two others drowned near the head of <a href="/wiki/Marble_Canyon" title="Marble Canyon">Marble Canyon</a>. The expedition was restarted by Stanton from <a href="/wiki/Dirty_Devil_River" title="Dirty Devil River">Dirty Devil River</a> (a tributary of <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon" title="Glen Canyon">Glen Canyon</a>) on November 25 and traveled through the Grand Canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Lohman1981_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lohman1981-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The expedition reached the Gulf of California on April 26, 1890 but the railroad was never built.</p>
<p>Prospectors in the 1870s and 1880s staked <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> claims in the canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> They hoped that previously discovered deposits of asbestos, copper, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> would be profitable to mine. Access to and from this remote region and problems getting <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> out of the canyon and its <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> made the whole exercise not worth the effort. Most moved on, and some stayed to seek profit in the tourist trade. Their activities did improve pre-existing Indian trails, such as <a href="/wiki/Bright_Angel_Trail" title="Bright Angel Trail">Bright Angel Trail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-APN_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APN-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:First_run_of_Grand_Canyon_Railway.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/37/First_run_of_Grand_Canyon_Railway.jpg/220px-First_run_of_Grand_Canyon_Railway.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_run_of_Grand_Canyon_Railway.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A group photo of passengers who rode on the first run of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_Railway" title="Grand Canyon Railway">Grand Canyon Railway</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A rail line to the largest city in the area, <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff,_Arizona" title="Flagstaff, Arizona">Flagstaff</a>, was completed in 1882 by the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_Railroad" title="Santa Fe Railroad" class="mw-redirect">Santa Fe Railroad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stage_coach" title="Stage coach" class="mw-redirect">Stage coaches</a> started to bring <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourists</a> from Flagstaff to the Grand Canyon the next year—an eleven-hour journey. Tourism greatly increased in 1901 when a spur of the Santa Fe Railroad to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_Village" title="Grand Canyon Village" class="mw-redirect">Grand Canyon Village</a> was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The first scheduled train with paying passengers of the Grand Canyon Railway arrived from <a href="/wiki/Williams,_Arizona" title="Williams, Arizona">Williams, Arizona</a>, on September 17 that year.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The 64-mile (103&#160;km) long trip cost $3.95, and naturalist <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> later commended the railroad for its limited environmental impact.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The first automobile was driven to the Grand Canyon in 1902. Oliver Lippincott from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California" class="mw-redirect">Los Angeles, California</a>, drove his <a href="/w/index.php?title=Toledo_Automobile_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Toledo Automobile Company (page does not exist)">Toledo Automobile Company</a>-built car to the South Rim from Flagstaff. Lippincott, a guide and two writers set out on the afternoon of January 4, anticipating a seven-hour journey. Two days later, the hungry and dehydrated party arrived at their destination; the countryside was just too rough for the ten-<a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a> (7&#160;kW) auto. A three-day drive from <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> in 1907 was required to reach the North Rim for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Competition with the automobile forced the Santa Fe Railroad to cease operation of the Grand Canyon Railway in 1968 (only three passengers were on the last run). The railway was restored and service reintroduced in 1989, and it has since carried hundreds of passengers a day. Trains remained the preferred way to travel to the canyon until they were surpassed by the auto in the 1930s. By the early 1990s more than a million automobiles per year visited the park. <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">Air pollution</a> from those vehicles and wind-blown pollution from Flagstaff and even the <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas</a> area has reduced visibility in the Grand Canyon and vicinity.</p>
<p>West Rim Drive was completed in 1912. In the late 1920s the first rim-to-rim access was established by the North Kaibab suspension bridge over the Colorado River.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Paved roads did not reach the less popular and more remote North Rim until 1926, and that area, being higher in elevation, is closed due to winter weather from November to April. Construction of a road along part of the South Rim was completed in 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Accommodations">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Accommodations">Accommodations</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_D._Lee.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/John_D._Lee.jpg/170px-John_D._Lee.jpg" width="170" height="238" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_D._Lee.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
John D. Lee (Utah State Historical Society)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_D._Lee" title="John D. Lee">John D. Lee</a> was the first person who catered to travelers to the canyon. In 1872 he established a <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> service at the confluence of the Colorado and <a href="/wiki/Paria_River" title="Paria River">Paria</a> rivers. Lee was in hiding, having been accused of leading the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Meadows_massacre" title="Mountain Meadows massacre">Mountain Meadows massacre</a> in 1857. He was tried and executed for this crime in 1877. During his trial he played host to members of the Powell Expedition who were waiting for their photographer, Major James Fennemore, to arrive (Fennemore took the last photo of Lee sitting on his own <a href="/wiki/Coffin" title="Coffin">coffin</a>). Emma, one of Lee's nineteen wives, continued the ferry business after her husband's death. In 1876 a man named <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harrison_Pierce&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harrison Pierce (page does not exist)">Harrison Pierce</a> established another ferry service at the western end of the canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The two-room <a href="/w/index.php?title=Farlee_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Farlee Hotel (page does not exist)">Farlee Hotel</a> opened in 1884 near Diamond Creek and was in operation until 1889. That year <a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Boucher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Louis Boucher (page does not exist)">Louis Boucher</a> opened a larger <a href="/wiki/Hotel" title="Hotel">hotel</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dripping_Springs" title="Dripping Springs">Dripping Springs</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Hance" title="John Hance">John Hance</a> opened his <a href="/wiki/Ranch_(land)" title="Ranch (land)" class="mw-redirect">ranch</a> near Grandview to tourists in 1886 only to sell it nine years later in order to start a long career as a Grand Canyon guide (in 1896 he also became local postmaster).</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Wallace_Bass.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/61/William_Wallace_Bass.jpg" width="100" height="130" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Wallace_Bass.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
William Wallace Bass</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wallace_Bass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William Wallace Bass (page does not exist)">William Wallace Bass</a> opened a tent house campground in 1890. Bass Camp had a small central building with common facilities such as a <a href="/wiki/Kitchen" title="Kitchen">kitchen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dining_room" title="Dining room">dining room</a>, and sitting room inside. Rates were $2.50 a day, and the complex was 20 miles (30&#160;km) west of the Grand Canyon Railway's Bass Station (Ash Fort). Bass also built the stage coach road that he used to carry his patrons from the train station to his hotel. A second Bass Camp was built along the Shinumo Creek drainage.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Canyon_Hotel_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grand Canyon Hotel Company (page does not exist)">Grand Canyon Hotel Company</a> was incorporated in 1892 and charged with building services along the stage route to the canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon31_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon31-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1896 the same man who bought Hance's Grandview ranch opened <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_Angel_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bright Angel Hotel (page does not exist)">Bright Angel Hotel</a> in Grand Canyon Village.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon31_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon31-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Cameron Hotel opened in 1903, and its owner started to charge a toll to use Bright Angel Trail.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon31_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon31-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:El_Tovar_Hotel_in_early_1900s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/El_Tovar_Hotel_in_early_1900s.jpg/220px-El_Tovar_Hotel_in_early_1900s.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:El_Tovar_Hotel_in_early_1900s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The El Tovar Hotel in the 1900s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Things changed in 1905 when the luxury <a href="/wiki/El_Tovar_Hotel" title="El Tovar Hotel">El Tovar Hotel</a> opened within steps of the Grand Canyon Railway's terminus.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> El Tovar was named for Don Pedro de Tovar who tradition says is the Spaniard who learned about the canyon from <a href="/wiki/Hopi" title="Hopi">Hopis</a> and told Coronado. Charles Whittlesey designed the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_crafts" title="Arts and crafts">arts and crafts</a>-styled rustic hotel complex, which was built with logs from <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and local stone at a cost of $250,000 for the hotel and another $50,000 for the <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stables</a> (a huge sum in 1905).<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon31_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon31-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> El Tovar was owned by Santa Fe Railroad and operated by its chief concessionaire, the Fred Harvey Company.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fred_Harvey_(entrepreneur)" title="Fred Harvey (entrepreneur)">Fred Harvey</a> hired <a href="/wiki/Mary_Elizabeth_Jane_Colter" title="Mary Elizabeth Jane Colter" class="mw-redirect">Mary Elizabeth Jane Colter</a> in 1902 as company architect. She was responsible for five buildings at the Grand Canyon: <a href="/wiki/Hopi_House" title="Hopi House">Hopi House</a> (1905), <a href="/wiki/Lookout_Studio" title="Lookout Studio">Lookout Studio</a> (1914), <a href="/wiki/Hermit%27s_Rest" title="Hermit's Rest">Hermit's Rest</a> (1914), <a href="/wiki/Desert_View_Watchtower" title="Desert View Watchtower">Desert View Watchtower</a> (1932), and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_Angel_Lodge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bright Angel Lodge (page does not exist)">Bright Angel Lodge</a> (1935).<sup id="cite_ref-APN_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APN-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> She stayed with the company until her retirement in 1948.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Cable_car" title="Cable car">cable car</a> system spanning the Colorado went into operation at Rust's Camp, located near the mouth of Bright Angel Creek, in 1907. Former U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> stayed at the camp in 1913. That, along with the fact that while president he declared Grand Canyon a <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Monument" title="U.S. National Monument" class="mw-redirect">U.S. National Monument</a> in 1908, led to the camp being renamed Roosevelt's Camp. In 1922 the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> gave the facility its current name, <a href="/wiki/Phantom_Ranch" title="Phantom Ranch">Phantom Ranch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon31_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon31-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In 1917 on the North Rim, W.W. Wylie built accommodations at Bright Angel Point.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_Lodge" title="Grand Canyon Lodge">Grand Canyon Lodge</a> opened on the North Rim in 1928. Built by a subsidiary of the <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific Railroad</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Parks_Company" title="Utah Parks Company">Utah Parks Company</a>, the lodge was designed by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Stanley_Underwood" title="Gilbert Stanley Underwood">Gilbert Stanley Underwood</a> who was also the architect for the <a href="/wiki/Ahwahnee_Hotel" title="Ahwahnee Hotel">Ahwahnee Hotel</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Valley" title="Yosemite Valley">Yosemite Valley</a>. Much of the lodge was destroyed by fire in the winter of 1932, and a rebuilt lodge did not open until 1937. The facility is managed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=TW_Recreation_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="TW Recreation Services (page does not exist)">TW Recreation Services</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TheGrandCanyon30_21-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheGrandCanyon30-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_Angel_Lodge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bright Angel Lodge (page does not exist)">Bright Angel Lodge</a> and the Auto Camp Lodge opened in 1935 on the South Rim.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span></h3>
<p>New <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a> trails, along old Indian trails, were established during this time as well. The world famous <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mule</a> rides down Bright Angel Trail were mass-marketed by the El Tovar Hotel. By the early 1990s, 20,000 people per year made the journey into the canyon by mule, 800,000 by hiking, 22,000 passed through the canyon by raft, and another 700,000 tourists fly over it in air tours (<a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a>). Overflights were limited to a narrow corridor in 1956 after two planes crashed, killing all on board. In 1991 nearly 400 <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescues</a> were performed, mostly for unprepared hikers who suffered from <a href="/wiki/Heat_exhaustion" title="Heat exhaustion" class="mw-redirect">heat exhaustion</a> and dehydration while ascending from the canyon (normal exhaustion and injured <a href="/wiki/Ankle" title="Ankle">ankles</a> are also common in rescuees).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a> theater just outside the park shows a reenactment of the Powell Expedition.</p>
<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kolb_Brothers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kolb Brothers (page does not exist)">Kolb Brothers</a>, Emery and Ellsworth, built a <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photographic</a> studio on the South Rim at the trailhead of Bright Angel Trail in 1904. Hikers and <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mule</a> caravans intent on descending down the canyon would stop at the <a href="/wiki/Kolb_Studio" title="Kolb Studio">Kolb Studio</a> to have their photos taken. The Kolb Brothers processed the prints before their customers returned to the rim. Using the newly invented <a href="/w/index.php?title=Path%C3%A9_Bray_camera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pathé Bray camera (page does not exist)">Pathé Bray camera</a> in 1911–12, they became the first to make a <a href="/wiki/Motion_picture" title="Motion picture" class="mw-redirect">motion picture</a> of a river trip through the canyon that itself was only the eighth such successful journey. From 1915 to 1975 the film they produced was shown twice a day to tourists with Emery Kolb at first narrating in person and later through tape (a feud with Fred Harvey prevented pre-1915 showings).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Grand_Canyon_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Protection efforts">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Protection_efforts">Protection efforts</span></h2>
<p>By the late 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation movement</a> was increasing national interest in preserving natural wonders like the Grand Canyon. <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Park" title="U.S. National Park" class="mw-redirect">National Parks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Valley" title="Yosemite Valley">Yosemite Valley</a> were established by the early 1890s. U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">Benjamin Harrison</a> introduced a bill in 1887 to establish a national park at the Grand Canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p397_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p397-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The bill died in committee, but on February 20, 1893, Harrison (then <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>) declared the Grand Canyon to be a National Forest Preserve.<sup id="cite_ref-ArchiveRecords_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveRecords-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> were allowed, but the designation did offer some protection.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p397_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p397-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> visited the Grand Canyon in 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> An avid outdoorsman and staunch conservationist, he established the Grand Canyon Game Preserve on November 28, 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-ArchiveRecords_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveRecords-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Livestock <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> was reduced, but predators such as <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">mountain lions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf" class="mw-redirect">wolves</a> were eradicated. Roosevelt added adjacent <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Forest" title="United States National Forest">national forest</a> lands and re-designated the preserve a <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Monument" title="U.S. National Monument" class="mw-redirect">U.S. National Monument</a> on January 11, 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-ArchiveRecords_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveRecords-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> Opponents, such as holders of land and mining claims, blocked efforts to reclassify the monument as a <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Park" title="U.S. National Park" class="mw-redirect">National Park</a> for 11 years. Grand Canyon National Park was finally established as the 17th U.S. National Park by an Act of Congress signed into law by President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> on February 26, 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-ArchiveRecords_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveRecords-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> declared the Fred Harvey Company to the official park concessionaire in 1920 and bought <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wallace_Bass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William Wallace Bass (page does not exist)">William Wallace Bass</a> out of business.</p>
<p>An area of almost 310 square miles (800&#160;km²) adjacent to the park was designated as a second Grand Canyon National Monument on December 22, 1932.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p395_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p395-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Marble Canyon National Monument was established on January 20, 1969, and covered about 41 square miles (105&#160;km²).<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p395_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p395-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> An act signed by President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> on January 3, 1975, doubled the size of Grand Canyon National Park by merging these adjacent national monuments and other federal land into it. That same act gave <a href="/w/index.php?title=Havasu_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Havasu Canyon (page does not exist)">Havasu Canyon</a> back to the <a href="/wiki/Havasupai" title="Havasupai">Havasupai</a> tribe.<sup id="cite_ref-Tufts1998p13_19-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tufts1998p13-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> From that point, the park stretched along a 278-mile (447&#160;km) segment of the Colorado River from the southern border of <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon_National_Recreation_Area" title="Glen Canyon National Recreation Area">Glen Canyon National Recreation Area</a> to the eastern boundary of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mead_National_Recreation_Area" title="Lake Mead National Recreation Area">Lake Mead National Recreation Area</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiver1999p395_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiver1999p395-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Grand Canyon National Park was designated a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> on October 24, 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1935, <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Dam" title="Hoover Dam">Hoover Dam</a> started to impound <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mead" title="Lake Mead">Lake Mead</a> south of the canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris1997_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris1997-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Conservationists lost a battle to save upstream <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon" title="Glen Canyon">Glen Canyon</a> from becoming a <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon_Dam" title="Glen Canyon Dam">Glen Canyon Dam</a> was completed in 1966 to control flooding and to provide water and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power" class="mw-redirect">hydroelectric power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Seasonal variations of high flow and flooding in the spring and low flow in summer have been replaced by a much more regulated system. The much more controlled Colorado has a dramatically reduced sediment load, which starves beaches and <a href="/wiki/Sand_bar" title="Sand bar" class="mw-redirect">sand bars</a>. In addition, clearer water allows significant <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> growth to occur on the riverbed, giving the river a green color.</p>
<p>With the advent of commercial flight, the Grand Canyon has been a popular site for aircraft overflights. However, a series of accidents resulted in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Overflights_Act_of_1987&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Overflights Act of 1987 (page does not exist)">Overflights Act of 1987</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, which banned flights below the rim and created flight-free zones.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The tourist flights over the canyon have also created a <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise problem</a>, so the number of flights over the park has been restricted.</p>
